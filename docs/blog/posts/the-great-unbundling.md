# The Great Unbundling

## Why AI agents are quietly dismantling the SaaS economy — and forcing the Mag 7 to pivot before the window closes

There's a quiet assumption baked into today's tech narrative: that the Mag 7 have already won. That their scale, distribution, and balance sheets make them untouchable in the age of AI.

I think the opposite is true.

<!-- more -->

They have time. What they do with that time is what decides whether they stay dominant or become the next generation of legacy infrastructure. Because the ground beneath them is shifting in a very specific way:

**AI isn't a feature. It's a new economic model.**

---

## SaaS was built for humans. AI is not.

The SaaS model is simple at its core: charge per user, per month, for software that amplifies human productivity.

It made perfect sense in a world where work was done by people, software was the tool, and scale meant more seats.

Agents break that equation.

When two agents can do the work of fifty users, you don't need fifty licenses. You don't need ten. In the traditional sense, you might not need any. The pricing model collapses first. The product model follows.

This is the real reason behind the slow, persistent compression in SaaS multiples — not that software is less valuable, but that the *unit* of value has changed.

---

## From seats to tokens

What replaces per-user pricing? Tokenisation — not in the crypto sense, but in the economic one.

You pay for compute. You pay for outcomes. You pay for execution, not access.

Agents don't care about seats. They care about tokens processed, tasks completed, workflows executed. So the future pricing unit looks less like *cost per employee* and more like *cost per task, cost per workflow, cost per decision*.

That's a problem if your entire revenue engine is built on expanding seat count.

---

## The agent layer is the new interface

Here's the deeper disruption: **SaaS tools are no longer the interface. Agents are.**

Instead of a human logging into five different systems, the agent orchestrates across them — pulling data, executing actions, returning outcomes.

In that world, the UI becomes irrelevant. The product becomes an API. The brand becomes invisible.

SaaS risks becoming commoditised infrastructure unless it owns — or embeds itself inside — the agent layer.

---

## Why the Mag 7 still have a window

This is where the incumbents still have the edge. They control compute, distribution, developer ecosystems, and enterprise trust. More importantly, they have time and cash flow to spend.

They can collapse pricing models before being forced to. They can bundle AI into existing ecosystems. They can acquire agent-native platforms. They can re-architect their products around workflows instead of features.

The real question is whether they will. Incumbents rarely fail from lack of resources. They fail from protecting yesterday's margins.

---

## The strategic fork

Every major platform now faces the same choice.

**Option 1: Defend SaaS economics.** Preserve per-seat pricing. Add AI as a premium feature. Optimise for short-term revenue.

**Option 2: Embrace the agent economy.** Shift to usage- or outcome-based pricing. Build native agent orchestration. Accept short-term revenue disruption for long-term dominance.

History is pretty clear about which path wins.

---

## What the next five years look like

Expect a sharp decline in per-seat pricing as the dominant model. Expect a wave of SaaS companies repositioning as "AI platforms" — some convincingly, most not. Expect agents to become the primary consumers of software, not humans. And expect a new layer of companies to emerge that sit *above* SaaS and orchestrate it.

Most importantly, expect consolidation. Once software becomes interchangeable infrastructure, scale matters again — but in a very different way.

---

## The final thought

The current narrative is that AI will augment SaaS. That's too optimistic.

AI doesn't just enhance tools. It changes who — or *what* — uses them. And when the user changes from human to agent, everything from pricing to product design has to change with it.

The Mag 7 aren't too big to fail.

But they are big enough to pivot.

The clock is ticking.
